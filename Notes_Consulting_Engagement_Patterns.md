STRUCTURAL PROTECTIONS FOR FUTURE ENGAGEMENTS

1. PAID DISCOVERY AS THE ENTRY POINT

   The SOW has "Phase 1: Discovery" at 4 hours ($300). This should
   be the FIRST paid engagement, not something that happens after
   hours of free work.

   Reframe: "I'd like to do a paid discovery session to understand
   your needs before I write a detailed proposal. It's 2-4 hours
   at $75/hour. At the end, you'll have a clear scope document
   whether or not we proceed."

   This does three things:
     - Tests whether they'll actually pay (filters tire-kickers)
     - Gets paid for research that would happen anyway
     - Provides a natural exit point if it's not a fit

2. THE "SKETCH VS. BLUEPRINT" DISTINCTION

   What to give away free:
     - A sketch
     - A one-page concept
     - Enough to show understanding of the problem

   What to charge for:
     - The blueprint
     - Detailed specs, hardware research, compliance analysis
     - Architecture decisions, deployment diagrams

   Tonight's work was the blueprint, not the sales pitch.

3. TIME-BOXED PROPOSALS

   Set a hard limit on pre-contract work. Example: "I spend a
   maximum of 2 hours on any proposal. If it needs more research
   than that, the research is billable."

   When the limit is reached: "I've done initial research and
   think this is feasible. To give you a detailed spec, I'd need
   to do a paid discovery phase. Here's what that looks like."

4. RETAINER FOR ONGOING ADVISORY

   When follow-up questions come in after the initial proposal
   (like the hardware quote request), that's the moment to say:
   "Happy to dig into that. I'll send you a short engagement
   letter for the research — probably 2-4 hours."

   The proposal was the free part. Everything after is paid.

THE TRUST-BUILDING PROBLEM

Fear: If I don't give them everything upfront, they won't trust
me enough to hire me.

Reality:
  - Clients who need the entire blueprint before committing often
    keep asking for more forever
  - Professionals in other fields (lawyers, architects, doctors)
    don't give away expertise to prove they have it
  - The SOW and budget addendum ARE the trust-building artifacts
  - The hardware BOM, deployment diagrams, and compliance research
    are deliverables, not sales materials

The question is not "how do I build enough trust?" It's "what is
the minimum viable trust, and how do I get paid for the rest?"

The key: detailed work happens in PAID DISCOVERY, not in the free
proposal phase.

   "I've put significant time into this proposal because I believe
   in the project. Going forward, I want to make sure we're on the
   same page about scope — the SOW covers the build, and any
   additional research or specification work would be billed as
   discovery."

   This isn't confrontational. It's professional. It sets the
   expectation for this engagement and any future work.

NAVIGATING THE INTERMEDIARY (CLARK / KYLIE / EXPRESS)

Clark and Kylie are a staffing/recruiting intermediary, not the
end client. This adds a layer:

  - They need to look good to EMO Oregon
  - They may not fully understand the technical scope
  - They're evaluating whether to refer more work in the future
  - Their incentive is to close the deal, not to protect my time

How to work with this:

  DO:
    - Make them look competent to EMO Oregon (clear docs help)
    - Give them materials they can forward confidently
    - Be responsive and professional
    - Understand their role in the relationship

  DON'T:
    - Let them scope-creep on EMO's behalf without compensation
    - Assume they'll protect my boundaries (that's my job)
    - Over-deliver to impress them at my own expense
    - Confuse their enthusiasm with a signed contract

  ASK IN THE MEETING:
    - "What does EMO need to see to make a decision?"
    - "Is there a timeline for approval?"
    - "Who signs the contract — EMO directly or through Express?"
    - "How do you typically handle scope changes mid-project?"

ENNEAGRAM 9 PATTERNS TO WATCH

The 9's gift: seeing all perspectives, creating harmony.
The 9's trap: disappearing into others' needs, losing own center.

In consulting, this shows up as:
  - Saying yes to scope creep to avoid conflict
  - Underpricing to avoid seeming greedy
  - Over-delivering to be liked
  - Avoiding the money conversation because it's uncomfortable

The antidote is STRUCTURES that protect against these tendencies:
  - Time limits
  - Written scope
  - Paid discovery
  - Contracts before work

These aren't adversarial — they create clarity so generosity can
happen within defined boundaries.

The phrase to practice:
  "I'd be happy to dig into that. Let me send you a short
   engagement letter."

RIGHTING THE SHIP: TOMORROW'S STANCE

Going into the meeting:

  1. Be warm but clear. The proposal is comprehensive. It's time
     for a decision, not more free iteration.

  2. If they want changes to the hardware options or scope, frame
     it as Phase 1 discovery work: "I can research that — let's
     kick off Phase 1 and I'll have options for you within a week."

  3. If they're not ready to decide, ask what they need and when
     they'll have it. Don't offer to do more free work to help
     them decide.

  4. Remember: a good client respects boundaries. A client who
     needs endless free work to commit is a client who will be
     difficult throughout the engagement.

  5. The intermediary relationship (Express) is an opportunity:
     if this goes well, there may be more referrals. That's a
     reason to do good work, not a reason to work for free.
